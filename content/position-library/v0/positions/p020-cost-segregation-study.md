## Position statement

A taxpayer owning a rental real property building may commission an engineering-based **cost segregation study** to identify components of the building qualifying as §1245 personal property (5-year or 7-year MACRS) or land improvements (15-year MACRS), rather than treating the entire building as §1250 real property (27.5-year residential or 39-year commercial). This accelerates depreciation deductions significantly in early ownership years.

## Plain-English description

When you buy a rental property, default tax treatment depreciates the entire building over 27.5 years (residential) or 39 years (commercial). Land is non-depreciable.

Cost segregation breaks this down: an engineering firm performs a study identifying components that legally qualify as faster-depreciation classes:

- **5-year personal property** (§1245): carpeting, decorative lighting, removable wall partitions, specialty plumbing, kitchen equipment in commercial
- **7-year personal property** (§1245): furniture, office equipment
- **15-year land improvements**: parking lots, fences, landscaping, sidewalks, signs, decorative pools
- **27.5/39-year real property** (§1250): structural elements, HVAC, foundations, roof, exterior walls, windows

Typical cost segregation results:
- 20-30% of building cost reclassified to 5-year or 15-year property
- For a $1M commercial property, that's $200-300K eligible for accelerated depreciation
- Combined with §168(k) bonus depreciation (60% for 2024, 40% for 2025, 20% for 2026, 0% for 2027), this triggers a huge first-year deduction
- Net first-year tax savings: $50K-$150K typical for $1M+ properties

**Critical requirements**:
1. **Engineering study** by qualified firm. IRS Audit Technique Guide on Cost Segregation requires a "quality study" with specific elements (CSP / Cost Segregation Study Procedures).
2. **§1245 vs §1250 classification** must be legally defensible per Hospital Corporation of America (Tax Court 1997) framework + subsequent case law.
3. **Bonus depreciation phaseout** (TCJA §168(k)) reduces the year-one impact: 60% bonus in 2024, 40% in 2025, 20% in 2026, 0% in 2027.
4. **Recapture on sale** under §1245(a)(2) — depreciation on personal property is recaptured at ordinary income rates on disposition (not the preferential 25% §1250 recapture).
5. **Look-back study** allowed for properties owned in prior years — file Form 3115 (Change of Accounting Method) per Rev. Proc. 2024-XX.

**Why Tier 1**: cost segregation is settled law (Hospital Corporation of America). The position itself is well-established.

**Why Tier 2-3 conditional**: the quality + defensibility of the engineering study is what determines audit success. Sloppy or DIY studies are common §6694(a) traps.

**Why an EA cares**: real-estate-investor clients with $500K+ properties routinely ask about cost segregation. Done right, it's $50K-$150K in first-year tax savings on a $1M property. Done wrong, it's a §6694(a) audit trigger.

## Fact pattern triggers

The Discovery agent should consider this position when:
- Schedule E rental with substantial building basis (>$500K typically)
- Schedule E newly-acquired rental in tax year
- Form 4562 with §168(k) bonus depreciation or §179 on real-property components
- Property type: commercial, multi-family residential >5 units, mixed-use
- Owner has high tax bracket (cost seg benefit scales with marginal rate)
- Prior years owned but no cost segregation done (look-back opportunity)

## 4-tier confidence rationale

**Tier 1 (Settled)** when:
- Engineering study performed by qualified firm with documented credentials
- Study includes detailed take-offs, photos, construction documents
- §1245 vs §1250 classifications align with Hospital Corp + subsequent case law
- Form 4562 + Form 3115 properly filed
- Bonus depreciation phaseout properly applied for the tax year

**Tier 2 (Substantial Authority)** when:
- Study is engineering-based but newer / less-tested methodology
- Component classifications include some borderline categories (e.g., specialty plumbing in residential)

**Tier 3 (Reasonable Basis + 8275)** when:
- Study has documentation gaps
- Components claimed are aggressive but with case-law support

**REFUSED below RB**:
- "DIY" cost segregation (no qualified engineer, just preparer's allocation)
- Components classified as §1245 that are clearly structural (foundation, exterior walls)
- §1245 classifications based on "rule-of-thumb" rather than engineering analysis
- Look-back without Form 3115 (Change of Accounting Method required)

## Required substantiation

1. **Engineering cost segregation study** with:
   - Firm credentials (engineering license, cost segregation specialization)
   - Detailed component identification with photos, drawings, construction documents
   - Cost allocations supported by replacement-cost methodology
   - §1245 vs §1250 classification reasoning per Hospital Corp framework
2. **Form 4562** with reclassified components
3. **Form 3115** if look-back to prior years (Change of Accounting Method)
4. **Depreciation schedules** by component (5-year, 15-year, 27.5/39-year)
5. **Bonus depreciation calculation** (60%/40%/20%/0% per year)
6. **Settlement statement / purchase agreement** documenting total acquisition cost
7. **Building component analysis** as study output

## Common audit-defense framing

When IRS challenges (cost segregation is in the IRS Audit Technique Guide; examiners are trained):

1. **Produce the engineering study** with all components
2. **Show §1245 vs §1250 classification reasoning** per Hospital Corp
3. **Cite the engineering firm's credentials**
4. **Apply bonus depreciation phaseout** correctly for tax year
5. **Show Form 3115** if look-back applicable

Typical IRS questions:
- "Was the study performed by a qualified engineer?"
- "What is the methodology for §1245 vs §1250 classification?"
- "Can you produce the component-level documentation?"
- "Was bonus depreciation correctly applied for the tax year?"
- "If look-back, was Form 3115 properly filed?"

Docket pre-logs each.

## Common mis-uses / failure modes

| Mis-use | Why it fails | Penalty |
|---|---|---|
| DIY cost seg (preparer allocates components without engineering study) | Fails Hospital Corp + IRS ATG quality-study standard | §6694(a) + §6662(a) |
| Classifying structural elements as §1245 personal property | §1245 doesn't apply to inherent structural components | §6694(a) |
| Mis-applying bonus depreciation phaseout (using 100% when phaseout has reduced to 60%) | §168(k) phaseout schedule | §6694(a) |
| Look-back without Form 3115 | Change of Accounting Method procedure required | §6694(a) + accounting-method consequences |
| Not recapturing §1245 depreciation on disposition | §1245 recapture is ordinary income, not §1250 25% rate | §6694(a) on disposition return |
| Cost seg on personal residence + rental conversion | Personal-use period doesn't qualify for §1245 acceleration | §6694(a) |
| Aggressive 5-year vs 7-year classifications | §168(e) recovery-period rules | §6694(a) |
| Cost seg on building without engineering documentation (just "rule of thumb") | Quality-study standard fails | §6694(a) + §6662 |
