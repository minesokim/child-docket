## Position statement

A California pass-through entity (S-Corp, partnership, certain LLCs) may elect to pay the **Pass-Through Entity Elective Tax** (PTET) at 9.3% of qualified net income under Cal. Rev. & Tax Code §17052.10. The PTET payment is deductible at the entity level as a state tax (avoiding the $10,000 federal SALT cap), and the entity's owners receive a refundable California PTET credit on their personal returns.

## Plain-English description

The federal SALT (State And Local Tax) deduction was capped at $10,000 by TCJA in 2017. For high-income California taxpayers, this dramatically increased federal tax. California's response (AB-150, 2021) was the PTET: a pass-through entity pays California income tax at the entity level (deductible as a federal state tax with no $10K cap), and the entity's owners get a CA credit for their share.

**The math** (simplified):
- S-Corp with $500K of CA-source income, 100% owner-managed
- Without PTET: owner pays CA personal income tax (~9.3% on $500K = $46,500), deducts up to $10K federally
- With PTET: S-Corp pays $46,500 to CA at the entity level (fully federally deductible), owner gets $46,500 CA credit on personal return; owner doesn't pay CA again

**Net federal tax savings**: roughly $46,500 × 37% (top federal bracket) = **$17,205/year for the $500K S-Corp**.

**Election mechanics**:
- Must elect by deadline (varies — see FTB Notice; typically June 15 of tax year for current year)
- Estimated PTET payment required by June 15 + balance by tax year filing date
- Election made on Form 100S, Form 568, or Form 565
- Credit claimed on owner's CA Schedule P or similar; Form 3804-CR

**Why Tier 1**: AB-150 is settled California law; mechanics are clearly defined in Cal. R&T Code; PTET deduction at federal level is supported by IRS Notice 2020-75. No substantive uncertainty.

**Why an EA cares**: every CA-based S-Corp / partnership client with significant CA-source income should be evaluated for PTET. Missing the election costs the client $5K-$50K+ annually. PTET deadline-missed errors are a classic §6694(a) exposure — the position is statutory; missing the election is preparation negligence.

## Fact pattern triggers

The Discovery agent should consider this position when:
- Form 1120-S, Form 1065, or Form 568 (CA LLC) with CA-source income
- Owner's personal AGI in upper brackets (high SALT-cap exposure)
- Prior year did NOT elect PTET (potential first-time election)
- Election was made prior year (verify continued election + credit calculation)
- Multi-state pass-through with significant CA apportionment

## Common audit-defense framing

When CA FTB challenges (typically a credit reconciliation or election-timing issue):

1. **Produce election form** with filing-date stamp
2. **Produce payment receipts** for estimated + balance
3. **Show Form 3804** calculation matches payment
4. **Show Form 3804-CR** matches K-1 PTET allocation
5. **Cite Cal. R&T Code §17052.10 + IRS Notice 2020-75**

Typical FTB questions:
- "When was the PTET election filed?"
- "Where is the PTET payment documentation?"
- "Does the credit claimed match the entity's PTET payment?"
- "What is the apportionment for non-CA income?"

Docket pre-logs each.

## Common mis-uses / failure modes

| Mis-use | Why it fails | Penalty |
|---|---|---|
| Missing the election deadline | Election lost for the tax year; $5K-$50K+ in federal tax savings forgone | §6694(a) preparer negligence |
| Claiming PTET credit > entity's actual PTET payment | Substantive math error | §6694(a) + FTB reconciliation |
| Backdating PTET election | Election is irrevocable + must be timely | §6694(a)-(b) |
| Failing to make estimated PTET payment by June 15 | Election may be invalid or penalties apply | FTB late-payment penalty |
| Incorrect pro-rata allocation in multi-owner entity | K-1 mismatch | §6694(a) on multiple owners' returns |
| Claiming PTET on non-qualified pass-through (e.g., wrong entity type) | Eligibility fails | §6694(a) |
| Mis-applying multi-state apportionment | CA-source income mis-stated | §6694(a) |
