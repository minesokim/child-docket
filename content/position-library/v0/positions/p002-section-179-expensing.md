## Position statement

Section 179 allows a taxpayer to elect to immediately expense the cost of qualifying tangible §1245 property placed in service during the tax year, up to the annual statutory limit, rather than depreciating over the property's class life.

## Plain-English description

Section 179 is the statutory election that lets a small business write off the entire cost of qualifying equipment + certain real property improvements in the year of purchase, rather than depreciating over 5-39 years. The election is one of the most-used tax preferences in small-business prep.

**Key 2025 limits** (Rev. Proc. 2024-XX; 2026 amounts publish ~November 2025):
- **Expense limit**: $1,160,000 for 2025 (TCJA-set baseline of $1,000,000 indexed annually)
- **Phaseout threshold**: $2,890,000 for 2025 (begins reducing dollar-for-dollar above this)
- **Phaseout complete**: $4,050,000 for 2025 (no §179 deduction above this)
- **SUV limit**: $30,500 for 2025 (a separate cap on vehicles over 6,000 GVW within the 6,000-14,000 GVW range)

The election is made on Form 4562 Part I attached to the return. Carryforward applies if the deduction exceeds taxable income from the trade or business.

**Bonus depreciation interaction**: §179 is taken before §168(k) bonus depreciation. Bonus depreciation is phasing out (60% for 2024, 40% for 2025, 20% for 2026, 0% for 2027+), making §179 increasingly important.

**Why an EA cares**: this is among the highest-frequency elections in small-business preparation. Construction, real estate, retail, restaurants, and trades all routinely use §179. Mis-classifying property type or exceeding the per-business income limit are the common §6694(a) exposures.

## Fact pattern triggers

The Discovery agent should consider this position when ANY of the following appear:
- Form 1120-S, 1065, 1040 Schedule C, or Schedule F with depreciation deductions
- Form 4562 with depreciation entries (Part II/III but no Part I election)
- Asset purchases >$2,500 listed in fixed-asset schedules (note: under-$2,500 may use de minimis safe harbor per §1.263(a)-1(f))
- Vehicle purchases in business name
- Equipment, machinery, computer purchases
- Qualified Improvement Property (QIP) leasehold improvements
- Off-the-shelf software purchases
- Recent reform: §179 was expanded post-TCJA to include certain real-property improvements (roofs, HVAC, fire protection, alarm/security systems) per §179(f)

## 4-tier confidence rationale

This position is **Tier 1 (Settled law)** for nearly all fact patterns. §179 is a statutory election with well-defined limits. The math is the math.

**Where the tier might downgrade**:

- **Tier 2 (Substantial Authority)** if claiming §179 on certain qualified improvement property where the "placed in service" date is at issue — e.g., a leasehold improvement spanning two tax years.
- **Tier 3 (Reasonable Basis + 8275)** if claiming §179 on property used <50% in business (treats §179 as recapture-triggering investment, requires careful analysis under §179(d)(10)).
- **REFUSED below RB**: claiming §179 on property that doesn't qualify under §179(d)(1) — e.g., land (never qualifying), or buildings classified as §1250 real property (only certain post-TCJA improvements qualify per §179(f)).

**Where Tier 1 holds**:
- Equipment, machinery, computers, software (off-the-shelf)
- Vehicles with GVW > 14,000 lbs (no SUV cap)
- Furniture used in business
- Most qualifying real property improvements per §179(f)

## Required substantiation

1. **Form 4562 Part I** with the §179 election clearly stated
2. **Asset purchase invoices** showing date, vendor, description, total cost
3. **Placed-in-service date documentation** (delivery date + readiness-for-use date if different)
4. **Business-use percentage** (especially for vehicles)
5. **Election statement** if first §179 election or aggregating multiple assets
6. **Recapture analysis** if business use drops below 50% in a later year (Form 4797 Part II)
7. **Carryover schedule** if §179 deduction exceeded taxable income from the trade or business

## Common audit-defense framing

When the IRS challenges a §179 deduction:

1. **Verify property qualifies under §179(d)(1)**: cite to specific subsection covering the asset type. Equipment is straightforward; real property improvements require §179(f) cite.
2. **Verify placed-in-service date is in the tax year claimed**: the "placed in service" test is the load-bearing piece (per §1.179-4(e) — property is placed in service when ready and available for its specifically assigned function).
3. **Verify business use >50%** (mandatory for the election; if listed property like vehicles, requires §280F substantiation).
4. **Verify the deduction doesn't exceed the business income limit per §179(b)(3)** — show the trade-or-business income computation.
5. **Verify phaseout calculation if total §179 property exceeds $2,890,000 (2025)**.

Typical IRS questions:
- "When was the property placed in service?"
- "What was the business-use percentage in the year of acquisition?"
- "What was the trade-or-business income for the year?"
- "Was the SUV limit applicable, and was it correctly applied?"
- "Was the recapture triggered in any subsequent year?"

## Common mis-uses / failure modes

| Mis-use | Why it fails | Penalty |
|---|---|---|
| Claiming §179 on land | Land never qualifies as §179 property | §6694(a) |
| Claiming §179 on a building (non-improvement) | Buildings are §1250 real property; only certain post-TCJA improvements qualify | §6694(a) |
| Exceeding the SUV cap on a 6,000-14,000 GVW vehicle | §179(b)(5) explicitly caps SUV deduction at $30,500 for 2025 | §6694(a) |
| Claiming §179 in a year with no business income | §179(b)(3) business income limit triggers carryover; deduction in the wrong year | §6694(a) if claimed knowingly |
| Failing to capture recapture when business use drops <50% in a later year | §179(d)(10) recapture is triggered | §6694(a) on subsequent return |
| Aggregating §179 deductions across unrelated businesses to exceed limit | Each "controlled group" under §179(b)(2)(E) shares one limit | §6694(a) |
| Treating personal-use property as business property | §1.179-2 requires business use; personal use disqualifies | §6694(a)-(b) |
