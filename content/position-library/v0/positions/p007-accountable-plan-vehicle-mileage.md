## Position statement

An S-Corp may reimburse its >2% shareholder or any employee for business mileage driven in a personal vehicle, under a §62(c) accountable plan, at the IRS standard mileage rate ($0.67/mile for 2025; rate updates annually per IRS Notice). The reimbursement is deducted by the S-Corp as a §162 ordinary business expense and excluded from the shareholder's gross income under §1.62-2.

## Plain-English description

When an S-Corp shareholder uses their personal vehicle for business purposes, the cleanest mechanism to capture the cost is the accountable plan reimbursement at the IRS standard mileage rate. The mechanism:

1. S-Corp adopts an accountable plan policy (board minutes / employment agreement).
2. Shareholder tracks business miles via mileage log (date, business purpose, destination, miles).
3. Monthly: shareholder submits substantiation; S-Corp reimburses at standard rate.
4. S-Corp deducts reimbursement as §162 ordinary expense.
5. Shareholder excludes reimbursement from gross income under §1.62-2.

**2025 IRS standard mileage rate**: **$0.67/mile** (per IRS Notice 2024-XX; check 2026 Notice for updated rate).

**Why standard rate vs actual expense**:
- Standard rate: simple, automatic, no fuel/repair/depreciation tracking required, deductible at $0.67/mile regardless of actual cost
- Actual expense: more deductible if vehicle cost > $0.67/mile equivalent; requires receipts for fuel, repairs, depreciation, insurance pro rata

**Critical substantiation requirements** per Treas. Reg. §1.274-5(c):
1. Date of each business trip
2. Business purpose
3. Destination
4. Number of miles
5. Total mileage for the year (business + personal)
6. Time and place of travel
7. Business relationship of the people met

**Why Tier 1 vs Tier 3 conditional**:
- **Tier 1**: complete §1.274-5 substantiation maintained contemporaneously (e.g., MileIQ, Triplog, or paper mileage log)
- **Tier 3 + 8275**: gaps in substantiation that have been reconstructed retroactively (this is more common than ideal in real practice)
- **REFUSED below RB**: no log + no reconstruction possible; or knowingly inflating mileage

**Why an EA cares**: nearly every S-Corp shareholder client has vehicle business use. Substantiation gaps are common (people forget to log). Knowing where the Refusal Floor sits (no log + no reconstruction = refuse) protects the EA's PTIN.

## Fact pattern triggers

The Discovery agent should consider this position when:
- S-Corp 1120-S Line 17 with vehicle mileage reimbursement to shareholder
- Owner uses personal vehicle for business (mentioned in intake or visible from prior return)
- Mileage log exists (MileIQ, Triplog, paper) — substantiation potentially complete
- High business mileage relative to total miles driven
- Service-business or trades-business (construction, real estate, consulting) where site visits common

## 4-tier confidence rationale

**Tier 1 (Settled law)** when substantiation is complete:
- Contemporaneous mileage log (electronic or paper) covering >75% of business trips
- Standard mileage rate applied correctly
- Accountable plan policy documented
- Reimbursement reasonable (matches miles × rate)

**Tier 3 (Reasonable Basis + 8275)** when substantiation has gaps:
- Mileage log covers 50-75% of trips; remainder reconstructed from calendar + project schedule + client visits
- Reasonable reconstruction under §1.274-5(e) is defensible but examiners scrutinize
- Form 8275 disclosure recommended

**REFUSED below RB**:
- No log + no reconstruction possible (knowingly fabricated miles)
- Claiming personal commuting miles as business (Treas. Reg. §1.262-1(b)(5))
- Reimbursement rate above IRS standard rate without actual-expense substantiation

## Required substantiation

1. **Contemporaneous mileage log** with §1.274-5(c) elements:
   - Date of trip
   - Business purpose
   - Destination
   - Miles driven (business portion)
2. **Total annual mileage** (odometer year-start + year-end)
3. **Business-use percentage** (annual business miles / annual total miles)
4. **Accountable plan policy** (board minutes or employment agreement)
5. **Reimbursement records** (monthly submissions + payment receipts)
6. **Vehicle identification** (year, make, model, VIN — for vehicle assignment per §274(d))

## Common audit-defense framing

When IRS challenges:

1. **Show the mileage log** with §1.274-5(c) elements.
2. **Calculate business-use percentage** (Y business miles / Y+Z total miles).
3. **Show accountable plan policy** dated prior to reimbursement.
4. **Apply the IRS standard rate × miles** = reimbursement.
5. **Cite §1.274-5(e) reasonable reconstruction** if any gaps exist.

Typical IRS questions:
- "Where is the mileage log?"
- "What was the business purpose of each trip?"
- "What's the business-use percentage?"
- "Is commuting mileage included? If so, why?"
- "Was the accountable plan policy in place?"

Docket pre-logs each.

## Common mis-uses / failure modes

| Mis-use | Why it fails | Penalty |
|---|---|---|
| Claiming personal commuting miles as business | §1.262-1(b)(5) explicitly disallows commuting | §6694(a) |
| Reimbursing above $0.67/mi without actual-expense substantiation | Standard rate is a cap; excess requires actual-expense docs | §6694(a) |
| No mileage log + no reasonable reconstruction | §1.274-5 substantiation fails | §6694(a)-(b) |
| Backdated or fabricated mileage log | Substantive failure; potential §6694(b) | §6694(b); §7206 (willful) |
| Reimbursing without an accountable plan in place | Reimbursement becomes taxable wages per §1.62-2 | §6694(a) |
| Claiming standard rate AND actual-expense items (e.g., parking, tolls separately fine; but not fuel/repairs/depreciation) | Mixed method not allowed for the same vehicle | §6694(a) |
| Inflating business-use % (claiming 90% when actual is 50%) | Substantiation fails on examination | §6694(a)-(b) |
