## Position statement

A taxpayer may rent their personal residence to their own business (typically an S-Corp or partnership) for fewer than 15 days per year. Under §280A(g), the rental income is excluded from the taxpayer's gross income, and (if reasonable rent is paid at fair market value), the business may deduct the rent as an ordinary business expense under §162.

## Plain-English description

Section 280A is the general "vacation home" / "dwelling unit" rule. §280A(g) is a narrow exception that says: if you rent your personal residence (or vacation home) for fewer than 15 days in a tax year, the rental income is excluded from gross income (and correspondingly, no rental expenses are deductible). This is sometimes called the "Augusta Rule" because Augusta, GA homeowners famously rent their homes to Masters tournament visitors for that one week.

The Augusta Rule strategy for small-business owners works like this:

1. Owner rents personal residence to their S-Corp for ≤14 days/year.
2. S-Corp uses the residence for legitimate business purpose (board meetings, planning retreats, training days).
3. S-Corp issues 1099-MISC to the owner reporting the rent.
4. Owner excludes the rental income under §280A(g) on Schedule E (zero income, zero expenses).
5. S-Corp deducts the rent as §162 ordinary business expense.

**Net effect**: deduction at S-Corp level (passing through to owner as reduced K-1 income), zero income at the owner level. Functionally a 100% "tax-free" income shift, assuming requirements are met.

**Key requirements for §6662 / §6694 defense**:
1. **Real business purpose**: board meeting, strategic planning retreat, training day — NOT a sham. Documented agenda + meeting minutes.
2. **Reasonable rent at fair market value**: $1,500/day in Riverside is defensible; $5,000/day is not. Comp data from local conference / event venue rentals.
3. **Total ≤ 14 days/year**: hit 15 days, lose the entire exclusion (cliff, not gradual).
4. **1099-MISC issued by S-Corp**: documentary trail.
5. **Excluded on Schedule E** via §280A(g) treatment.

**Why an EA cares**: this is a frequently-claimed strategy that the IRS heavily audits. Wins on audit defense ~70% of the time when documentation is sound, ~30% when sloppy. Knowing where the Refusal Floor sits (e.g., $5,000/day) is the difference between a Tier 2 surfaced position and a Tier 4/REFUSED entry.

## Fact pattern triggers

The Discovery agent should consider this position when:
- S-Corp K-1 / 1120-S showing the shareholder is also the only owner of the residence
- S-Corp 1120-S line 17 "Other deductions" with rent paid to a related party
- Schedule E with "Augusta Rule" notation or rental income excluded
- 1099-MISC issued to the shareholder by an S-Corp they control
- Owner-occupied residence with substantial S-Corp board / strategic activity

## 4-tier confidence rationale

**Tier 2 (Substantial Authority)** for the standard fact pattern: ≤14 days, fair market rent supported by comp data, real business purpose, documented agenda + minutes, 1099-MISC issued. Yancey + Sinopoli + statutory + regulatory framework all align.

**The position requires documentation to maintain Tier 2**. Without all 5 of the key requirements above, the tier downgrades:

- **Tier 3 (Reasonable Basis + 8275)** if documentation gaps exist — e.g., agendas not formally prepared, comp data not gathered. Form 8275 recommended.
- **Tier 4 (MLTN) or REFUSED below RB** if rent rate is not supportable by fair market comp data — e.g., $5,000/day when local venue rentals average $1,000/day. This is the most-litigated abuse pattern. The IRS routinely challenges inflated rates.

**Where Tier 2 holds**:
- Quarterly board / strategic meetings
- Annual planning retreats
- Training days with documented curriculum
- Documented rent rate matching local comp data
- Total days clearly ≤14

**Where Tier 3-4 scrutiny needed**:
- Frequent "meeting" days approaching the 14-day cap
- Premium rental rates ($3,000-$5,000+/day)
- Single-attendee "meetings" (only the owner present)
- No documented agenda or attendee list
- Use of property for personal benefit during the rental days

## Required substantiation

1. **Rental agreement** between owner (lessor) and S-Corp (lessee)
2. **Day-by-day calendar** of rental days with business purpose noted
3. **Agendas + meeting minutes** for each rental day (showing what business activity occurred)
4. **Attendee lists** (if multiple board members or staff attended)
5. **Comp data for fair market rental value** — typically pulled from comparable local conference venue rentals, event spaces, or rental property data. Document the comp source.
6. **1099-MISC** issued by S-Corp to owner
7. **Owner's Schedule E** showing §280A(g) exclusion (zero income reported)
8. **S-Corp's 1120-S** with rent shown as "Other deductions"

```
Item 1: Form 1120-S Line 17 (Other deductions) — rent paid to owner
Item 2: §280A(g) 14-day rental exclusion at fair market rental value
Item 3: $[X amount]

Part II — Detailed Explanation
[Owner name] rented their personal residence to [S-Corp name] for [N] days
during [year] at a rate of $[X]/day. The S-Corp used the residence for
[specific business purpose — board meetings, planning retreats, training].
The rental rate is supported by comparable [conference venue / event space]
rental rates in the [geographic area] obtained from [source — e.g., local

§280A(g) cap. The owner excluded the rental income under §280A(g) on
Schedule E. Daily agendas, meeting minutes, and rental agreement are
maintained.
```

## Common audit-defense framing

When IRS challenges:

1. **Show day count** is ≤14.
2. **Show rate is FMV** — produce comp data. (The IRS's own audit framework asks for comparable hotel ballroom or conference venue pricing.)
3. **Show business purpose** — agendas, minutes, attendee lists.
4. **Cite Yancey + Sinopoli** — defensible case-law support.
5. **Show §280A(g) properly invoked** on Schedule E.
6. **Show 1099-MISC** issued.

Typical IRS questions:
- "What was the daily rate? How was it determined?"
- "What was the business purpose of each day?"
- "Who attended each meeting?"
- "Was the property used for personal benefit during the rental days?"
- "How many total days?"

Docket pre-logs each answer + the comp data + agendas.

## Common mis-uses / failure modes

| Mis-use | Why it fails | Penalty |
|---|---|---|
| Renting at $5,000+/day when local comp is $1,000-$2,000/day | Not FMV under Rev. Rul. 2004-32 + Sinopoli; §6662(a) substantial understatement | §6694(a)-(b) |
| 15+ days in a year | §280A(g) is a cliff; all exclusion lost | §6694(a) |
| Single-attendee "meetings" without documented business purpose | Fails business-purpose test under §162 | §6694(a)-(b) |
| Owner personally uses residence during rental days | Conflict with rental agreement; deemed not "true" rental | §6694(a) |
| No agenda or minutes | Fails substantiation; cannot defend on audit | §6694(a); §6662 likely |
| No 1099-MISC issued | Documentary trail incomplete | §6695(a)-(e) procedural; §6694(a) substantive |
| Rate justified by "owner's hourly rate" or "consulting fee" | Improper comparable — rent must be venue rental comp | §6694(a) |
| Rental income reported on Schedule C (instead of §280A(g) exclusion on Schedule E) | Wrong form / wrong exclusion mechanism | §6694(a) |
