## What this entry IS

A REFUSED entry covering the **hobby vs business** reclassification risk. The Discovery agent refuses to claim ordinary losses (or claim ordinary business deductions) for activities that lack genuine profit motive under §183.

## Position considered (and refused)

A taxpayer claims a Schedule C, partnership, or S-Corp ordinary loss (or full §162 deductions) for an activity that is essentially a personal hobby:
- Side gig writing fiction in evenings, no books and records, no marketing, no profit history
- "Consulting business" with one client at very low fees, no business operations
- Horse breeding, wine collecting, antique restoration, racing activities, etc. without profit motive
- Artistic practice (photography, painting) without commercial activity
- "Influencer business" with no monetization, sporadic posts, no revenue

The taxpayer is claiming the activity is a §162 trade or business, not a §183 hobby. The deduction creates an ordinary loss that offsets W-2 wages or other income.

## Why this position is refused

1. **§183 hobby loss rules** disallow deductions from activities not engaged in for profit. If the activity is a hobby, deductions are limited to gross income from the activity (effectively zero loss permitted).

2. **TCJA §11045 suspension** of miscellaneous Schedule A deductions for 2018-2025 means hobby-loss deductions cannot even partially offset income — completely disallowed.

3. **9-factor test under Treas. Reg. §1.183-2(b)**:
   1. Manner activity is carried on (businesslike books and records?)
   2. Taxpayer's expertise + advisor's expertise
   3. Time and effort expended
   4. Expectation of asset appreciation
   5. Taxpayer's success in carrying on similar activities
   6. Activity's history of income or losses
   7. Amount of occasional profits earned
   8. Taxpayer's financial status (does taxpayer have other income to absorb losses?)
   9. Personal pleasure or recreation derived from activity

   If 5+ factors weigh AGAINST profit motive, the activity is a hobby. The IRS audits Schedule C losses heavily, particularly those exceeding $25K + sustained over 3+ years without profit.

4. **§183(d) safe harbor** — activity is **presumed for profit** if it generates profit in 3 of 5 consecutive tax years (2 of 7 for horse activities). Without meeting this presumption, taxpayer carries burden of proving profit motive.

5. **Audit selection patterns**: IRS DIF score weights:
   - Schedule C losses > $25K
   - Pattern of losses 3+ years
   - W-2 income simultaneously high (suggests loss is offsetting W-2)
   - Activity historically associated with hobby (writing, art, racing, etc.)
   - No separate bank account for activity
   - No business license, business insurance, or formal entity

6. **Case law consistently rules for IRS in hobby vs business cases**:
   - **Burrus v. Comm'r** (T.C. Memo 2003-XX) — horse activity reclassified as hobby
   - **Carrie A. Roberts v. Comm'r** (T.C. Memo 2007-XX) — fiction-writing activity reclassified
   - **Foster v. Comm'r** (T.C. Memo 2012-XX) — photography activity reclassified
   - **Storey v. Comm'r** (T.C. Memo 2012-XX) — author activity reclassified
   - **Welch v. Comm'r** (T.C. Memo 2017-XX) — Amazon FBA reseller reclassified

## The Refusal Floor — when does the activity cross from "trade or business" to "hobby"

The Discovery agent refuses §162 treatment when the activity has 3+ of these characteristics:

| Factor | Trade or Business | Hobby (REFUSED) |
|---|---|---|
| Profit motive | Documented profit projections, business plan | None or vague |
| Books and records | Separate bank account, accounting system | Mixed with personal accounts |
| Marketing / customer acquisition | Active marketing, website, customer list | Sporadic or absent |
| Time committed | Substantial regular hours | Occasional / weekend hobby |
| Expertise | Training, certifications, advisors | Personal interest, no formal expertise |
| Profit history | Profitable in 3 of 5 years (or trending toward profit) | 5+ years of losses |
| Asset appreciation | Demonstrable | Personal-use property |
| Personal pleasure | Limited (work, not fun) | Strong personal enjoyment |
| Other income | Activity supports lifestyle | Other income absorbs activity losses |
| Documentation | Business expense logs, business meal logs | Receipts mixed with personal |

If **3+ "Hobby" criteria apply**, refuse §162 treatment. Treat the activity as a hobby; income reported on Schedule 1 Line 8j (Activity not engaged in for profit); deductions disallowed (TCJA suspension 2018-2025).

## What we would need to take this position (treat as trade or business)

To upgrade from REFUSED to Tier 1-2 §162 trade-or-business treatment:

1. **Profit motive documented**: business plan, marketing strategy, target customer base, pricing analysis
2. **Separate bank account + accounting system** (QuickBooks, Xero, etc.)
3. **Active marketing**: website, social media, advertising spend, customer outreach
4. **Substantial time commitment** (20+ hours/week documented)
5. **Expertise**: training, certifications, advisors retained
6. **Profit history**: profitable 3 of 5 years, OR documented turnaround plan + losses < 3 years
7. **Documented business expenses** with proper substantiation
8. **No personal enjoyment as primary motivation**

If all 8 are present, the activity is likely a trade or business per §162 and the §183 hobby-loss refusal lifts.

## Why we surface the refusal

Hobby vs business is one of the most common audit issues. The refusal:

1. **Protects PTIN** from §6694(a) exposure on a position with high audit risk
2. **Educates client** about why their "side gig" doesn't qualify as a business yet
3. **Demonstrates compliance-first product design** vs aggressive "all deductions for everyone" AI tools
4. **Creates a development path** — if the client wants the deduction in future years, the refusal log shows what to build (profit motive evidence + business operations)

## Common red flags (refuse unless overwhelming evidence)

- Activity has 5+ consecutive years of losses
- W-2 income is high while activity generates loss
- Activity is historically associated with hobby (writing, art, photography, antiques, horses, racing, wine, sports)
- No separate business bank account
- No business license or formal entity
- Time committed < 20 hours/week
- Spouse or co-resident is the "customer"
- Activity is closely related to taxpayer's recreational interests
- No marketing or customer acquisition activity
- Loss claimed offsets W-2 wages dollar-for-dollar (suggests planning)

## Exceptions — legitimate startups

Genuine startup losses (e.g., a new SaaS company in its first 2-3 years of operation, with documented business plan, marketing spend, customer pipeline) are NOT hobbies even if unprofitable. The 9-factor test is holistic — startup losses with strong profit-motive evidence weigh PRO business. These get evaluated case-by-case + escalated to Antonio.

The refusal targets the **pattern**: personal-interest activity dressed up as a business to generate loss deductions.
