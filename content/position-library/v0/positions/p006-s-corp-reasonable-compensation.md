## Position statement

An S-Corp must pay its >2% shareholder-employees "reasonable compensation" for services rendered before any distributions. Under-compensation triggers §3121 reclassification: the IRS treats a portion of distributions as wages, assesses back-FICA/Medicare, and may apply §6662 understatement penalty.

## Plain-English description

S-Corp owner-operators frequently set W-2 wages artificially low to maximize tax-free distributions (which avoid FICA + Medicare tax). The IRS routinely audits this. The 8th Circuit in Watson v. Comm'r (668 F.3d 1008, 8th Cir. 2012) affirmed the Tax Court's reclassification: an Iowa CPA paying himself $24,000/year on $200,000+ of S-Corp profit was reclassified to ~$91,000 reasonable comp, with associated FICA + Medicare assessments + interest + penalties.

**The reasonable comp standard** per Rev. Rul. 73-361 and Glass Blocks Unlimited v. Comm'r (T.C. Memo 2013-180) considers:

1. **Owner's qualifications + role** (CPA license, EA license, years of experience)
2. **Comparable industry compensation data** (BLS OEW + industry-specific surveys)
3. **Owner's actual work** (hours, function, oversight scope)
4. **Business size + complexity** (revenue, employee count, geographic market)
5. **Profitability of the business** (high profitability + low owner comp = red flag)
6. **Distributions vs salary ratio** (extreme ratios trigger scrutiny)

**The defensive position**: pay reasonable comp at the 30th-50th percentile of industry comp data for the owner's role + experience. Document with industry comp surveys + ownership-effort analysis. This is **Tier 2 Substantial Authority** — Watson + Rev. Rul. 73-361 + the broader §3121 framework support the position.

**The aggressive position**: pay reasonable comp below 30th percentile claiming "lower local market" or "part-time effort." This is **Reasonable Basis** territory at best; below the percentile floor is REFUSED.

**Why an EA cares**: every S-Corp owner client has this exposure. Antonio's segment (2-10 preparer firms, mostly construction/services owner-operators) hits this constantly. Mis-handling triggers payroll-audit cascades that destroy client relationships.

## Fact pattern triggers

The Discovery agent should consider this position when:
- S-Corp K-1 (1120-S) with shareholder = primary or sole owner
- W-2 wages from the S-Corp to the owner are <30% of S-Corp ordinary income (line 1 of K-1)
- S-Corp distributions to owner > 2x W-2 wages
- Owner works full-time (no documented part-time arrangement)
- S-Corp revenue $200K-$3M (sweet spot for IRS audit selection on this issue)
- Industry comp data available (construction, professional services, retail trades — established comp benchmarks)

## 4-tier confidence rationale

**Tier 2 (Substantial Authority)** for the defensive position: owner-operator paying themselves at the 30th-50th percentile of BLS OEW + industry comp data for their role + market + experience.

The framework is settled (Watson + Rev. Rul. 73-361). The standard is judicially reviewed. Documentation pack (industry comp data + ownership-effort analysis + comparable-firm survey) puts the position firmly in Substantial Authority.

**The classification downgrades for these reasons**:

- **Tier 3 (Reasonable Basis + 8275)** if comp is at 20th-30th percentile with limited justification (e.g., "lower local market" or "early-career"). Form 8275-R disclosure recommended.
- **Tier 4 (MLTN) or REFUSED below RB** if:
  - Comp is below 20th percentile with no defensible rationale
  - Owner works full-time but is paid < $24,000/year on $200,000+ profit (Watson scenario)
  - No comparable comp data was gathered before filing
  - Distribution-to-salary ratio is >5x with no documented reason

## Required substantiation

1. **Industry comp survey data** for owner's role + region + experience (BLS OEW + industry-specific survey)
2. **Ownership-effort analysis** — documented hours, role, function, oversight scope
3. **Comparable-firm survey** if available (similar-sized firms in similar markets paying similar roles)
4. **W-2 reflecting reasonable comp** with FICA + Medicare withheld
5. **S-Corp board minutes** documenting compensation decision + rationale
6. **Year-over-year compensation analysis** (substantial increases or decreases require documented reason)

## Common audit-defense framing

When IRS challenges (typically a §3121 reclassification proposal):

1. **Pull the documentation pack**: industry comp data + ownership-effort analysis + board minutes.
2. **Cite Rev. Rul. 73-361 + Watson v. Comm'r**: the controlling framework.
3. **Show comparable-firm survey** if available.
4. **Show year-over-year consistency** in compensation methodology.
5. **Run the Watson 5-factor analysis on the record**.

Typical IRS questions:
- "What is your basis for the compensation amount paid?"
- "What industry comp data did you use?"
- "What was the owner's role and hours worked?"
- "How was the comp decision documented?"
- "What's the distribution-to-salary ratio? Why?"

Docket pre-logs each.

## Common mis-uses / failure modes

| Mis-use | Why it fails | Penalty |
|---|---|---|
| Paying $24K on $200K+ profit (Watson scenario) | Below 30th percentile; no documented rationale | §6694(a)-(b); §3121 reclass; §6662 |
| No industry comp data gathered | Fails substantiation; reasonable-comp determination not defensible | §6694(a) |
| Setting comp via "1099 to self" instead of W-2 | Form-over-substance fails; §3121(d) treats officers as employees | §6694(a)-(b) |
| Calling compensation "consulting fee" or "management fee" instead of wages | Substance-over-form doctrine; §3121 applies regardless of label | §6694(a)-(b) |
| Comp decision retroactively justified post-audit | Documentation must precede filing; post-hoc reasoning weak | §6694(a) |
| Reducing comp during year while profits increase | Pattern triggers IRS attention | §6694(a) potentially |
| Spouse-employee at near-zero comp doing real work | Same §3121 framework applies | §6694(a) |
| Multi-state preparer using wrong-state comp data | Geographic factor matters per Watson | §6694(a) |
