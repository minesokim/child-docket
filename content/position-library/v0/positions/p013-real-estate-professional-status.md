## Position statement

A taxpayer qualifying as a real estate professional under IRC §469(c)(7) may treat rental real estate activities as **non-passive**, allowing rental losses to offset ordinary income (wages, business income) without the $25,000 §469(i) limitation. Without REPS status, rental losses are passive and limited.

## Plain-English description

§469 is the passive activity loss (PAL) rules. Rental real estate is by default treated as a "per se passive" activity. Losses can only offset passive income; excess losses are suspended.

There's a $25,000 active-participation allowance under §469(i), but it phases out between $100K and $150K AGI. For high-income taxpayers, this allowance is zero.

**§469(c)(7) Real Estate Professional Status (REPS)** is the escape hatch. If the taxpayer qualifies as a real estate professional AND materially participates in each rental activity, the activity is treated as non-passive. Losses fully offset ordinary income.

**The §469(c)(7) tests** (BOTH must be met):

1. **More than 50% of personal services in tax year are in real property trades or businesses** in which the taxpayer materially participates
2. **More than 750 hours of services during the tax year** in real property trades or businesses in which the taxpayer materially participates

**§469(c)(7)(C) — "real property trades or businesses"**:
- Development, redevelopment, construction, reconstruction
- Acquisition, conversion, rental, operation, management, leasing
- Brokerage

**Material participation** (separate test, per §469(h)) — most-commonly satisfied via:
- 500+ hours in the activity
- Substantially all participation in the activity
- 100+ hours + at least as much as any other person
- "Significant participation" aggregation test

**The IRS scrutinizes this position aggressively**. Common audit triggers:
- High W-2 income + REPS claim (full-time W-2 + real estate is hard to reconcile with "more than 50% in real estate")
- Spouse claims REPS while other spouse works full-time elsewhere (joint material participation is allowed; allocation matters)
- No contemporaneous time logs
- Hours look fabricated (e.g., exactly 751 hours)

**Why an EA cares**: high-income clients with rental real estate routinely want REPS. Without proper documentation, this is a §6694(a) trap. With proper documentation, the tax savings can be $50K-$500K annually.

## Fact pattern triggers

The Discovery agent should consider this position when:
- Schedule E with rental real estate losses
- AGI > $150K (phaseout zone for §469(i) means REPS is the only path)
- Spouse owns rental real estate (joint material participation election possible)
- Multiple rental properties owned (aggregation election possible per §469(c)(7)(A))
- Real-estate-related W-2 income or self-employment (broker, agent, contractor)
- Prior year REPS claimed — verify continuity

## 4-tier confidence rationale

**Tier 2 (Substantial Authority)** when:
- Contemporaneous time log maintained (calendar, project records, contractor logs)
- 750+ hours documented across the year
- Real-estate-related W-2 or business activity supporting "more than 50% of services" test
- Aggregation election filed if multiple properties
- Material participation in each activity documented separately

**Tier 3 (Reasonable Basis + 8275)** when:
- Time log was reconstructed post-year-end (calendar review, project records, less granular)
- 750+ hours total but evidence less specific per-property
- W-2 job in real estate but uncertain "more than 50% of services" given hours worked

**REFUSED below RB**:
- Taxpayer has 40-hour-per-week non-real-estate W-2 job + claims REPS
- No time log + no reconstructable records
- Hours claimed look fabricated (exactly 751; round numbers; no calendar evidence)
- Material participation not separately analyzed per property

## Required substantiation

1. **Contemporaneous time log** with date, activity, property, hours
2. **Calendar / project records** corroborating time log
3. **Aggregation election under §469(c)(7)(A)** if multiple properties (filed with original return; irrevocable absent FTB consent — once made, must be revoked formally)
4. **Material participation analysis per activity** (if no aggregation election)
5. **Personal-services-percentage analysis** (real estate hours / total work hours)
6. **W-2 / business-income records** for both spouses if joint return
7. **Property management records** (lease activity, repairs, capital improvements)
8. **Brokerage activity records** if applicable
9. **Real-estate-related professional credentials** (broker license, contractor license, etc.) supporting the "trade or business" claim

## Common audit-defense framing

When IRS challenges (preparer audit + taxpayer audit both common):

1. **Produce time log** with §1.469-5T(f) evidentiary elements
2. **Apply both tests**: >50% of services + >750 hours
3. **Document material participation** per activity (or aggregate election)
4. **Show real-estate-related trade or business** (W-2 or 1099 income supporting)
5. **Cite Hakkak / Penley / Pohoski** for the relevant fact-pattern guidance
6. **Run the per-day analysis** if hours challenged (showed up M-F evenings + weekends → realistic)

Typical IRS questions:
- "Show me the time log."
- "What was the taxpayer's primary job? How many hours/week?"
- "Was the 750-hour threshold met?"
- "Was an aggregation election filed?"
- "Show me material participation for [property X]."

Docket pre-logs each.

## Common mis-uses / failure modes

| Mis-use | Why it fails | Penalty |
|---|---|---|
| Full-time W-2 + claiming REPS | >50% of services test fails | §6694(a) + §469 reclass + §6662 |
| 750 hours total claimed but no breakdown per activity | Material participation per activity unclear | §6694(a) |
| No aggregation election; activities not separately analyzed for material participation | Each activity is separately passive | §6694(a) + §469 partial reclass |
| Time log reconstructed retroactively without contemporaneous support | §1.469-5T(f) substantiation weak | §6694(a) |
| Aggregation election filed mid-year | Irrevocable once filed; election must be timely | §6694(a) |
| Spouse with W-2 in unrelated field; REPS claimed jointly without analysis | Material participation per-spouse analysis required | §6694(a) |
| Brokerage activity claimed but no broker's license | "Real property trade or business" element fails | §6694(a) |
| Hours that suggest fabrication (e.g., exactly 751) | Substantive credibility fails on audit | §6694(b) potentially |
