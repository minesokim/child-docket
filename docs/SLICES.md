## Slice 2 — The Maria reframe (the load-bearing reframe)

This is the passage that broke the "tax research engine" framing and replaced it with practice management. Verbatim from the user:

> "Not exactly what we are building. But it is the foundation of what we are trying to build. We are trying to build an agentic first, practice management in a sense? What I shared with you is only half of the picture.
>
> Let me sit in the seat for a minute. Picture an EA. Her name is Maria. 58 years old, took the SEE in 2003 after working as a bookkeeper, runs her practice out of a small office next to a Pollo Loco in Riverside, has 240 active clients, mostly Latino small business owners and self-employed contractors. Her husband answers the phone. Her daughter helps during busy season. She uses OLTPro because that's what she learned. She charges $300 to $800 per return. She works 70 hours a week from February to April and 30 hours a week the rest of the year. She gets 50 WhatsApp messages a day from clients in Spanish. She is exhausted, proud, and quietly worried that the younger CPAs with the slick websites are eating her lunch.
>
> She does not need a better way to write a tax memo. She has never written a tax memo in her life. That's a CPA word.
>
> Here's what's actually happening in her day, and what would actually move her:
>
> **TaxGPT solves tasks. Docket solves the day.** This is the reframe. TaxGPT is a tool you open when you have a question. It sits there waiting. The whole product is built around moments of explicit invocation. But Maria's problem isn't that she can't research a tax question. Her problem is that she's running 240 simultaneous engagements in her head, dropping things, forgetting follow-ups, looking unprofessional when she has to ask the same client for the same W-2 twice. The currency isn't research speed. It's mental load. Morning Intelligence already gets at this but the framing should be louder. Docket is what she opens first thing in the morning to find out what today is. TaxGPT is what she opens when she's already in trouble.
>
> The product TaxGPT built is for CPAs. EAs are a different species. The TaxGPT website talks about memos, IRS notice responses, multi-jurisdiction SALT research, engagement letters. That is the language and workflow of a 12-person CPA firm doing advisory work. The actual Maria sends short text messages, takes payments via Zelle, and her 'engagement letter' is a one-page PDF her son made in 2019. The opportunity is to build for the EA reality that nobody else takes seriously: WhatsApp as primary client channel, Spanish-language client portals, payment links over Zelle and Venmo, voice memo transcription because half her client interactions are over the phone, simple templated messages instead of formal memos. Every CPA-flavored AI tool is a bad fit for this market. Nobody is building for it.
>
> TaxGPT sees queries. Docket sees the practice. This is huge and underexploited. TaxGPT can't tell Maria that she charged Nguyen $400 last year for the exact same scope she charged Patel $700. TaxGPT can't tell her that she spent 14 hours on Henderson last year for $450 and her effective hourly rate on that client was $32. TaxGPT can't tell her that 12 of her 1099 contractor clients still haven't sent in their W-9s and here's a one-click batch reminder. This is the consulting-engagement-level insight that no software gives a solo EA today, and it's worth $300/mo by itself. The pattern detection across the whole book is something only the system of record can do. TaxGPT is structurally not the system of record.
>
> The audit trail is not for auditors. It's for clients. Every EA has been burned by a client saying 'I told you to...' or 'I never received that...' Email is messy, voicemail is unsearchable, texts disappear. Docket's evidence linking and audit trail aren't just for compliance theater. They're armor. The pitch is: 'Every promise your client made, every document they sent, every approval they gave, timestamped and searchable. Next time someone says they told you something, you have receipts.' This emotionally lands because every EA has lived this story. TaxGPT cannot do this because it doesn't sit in the workflow.
>
> The fire-the-bad-client product. Every solo EA has 5 to 10 nightmare clients eating 30% of their time for 5% of revenue. They know this in their bones but can't quantify it. Cold numbers give permission. Docket can score every client by margin, friction, payment speed, scope creep. 'These five clients are dragging your effective rate below $40/hr. Here's a template for a respectful price increase that will either fix the relationship or end it.' This is therapy disguised as software. TaxGPT cannot touch this because they don't have the practice data.
>
> The proactive co-pilot for the inbox. Maria has 47 unread emails right now. Half are clients asking 'where's my refund,' 'did you file my extension,' 'when can I pick up my taxes.' A Docket inbox that pre-drafts replies pulled from actual client status (return is at e-file step 3, refund expected May 12 per IRS transcript) and lets her hit approve is worth more than the entire research feature set TaxGPT offers. Same with phone calls. Transcribe, summarize, draft, hit send. The phone tax is the single biggest hidden cost of running a practice and nobody is targeting it."

**Why this matters:** The Maria framing is illustrative; the real partner is Antonio at Vazant. But every italicized phrase above ("the currency is mental load," "armor against clients," "the phone tax," "the fire-the-bad-client product," "TaxGPT solves tasks; Docket solves the day") is product copy that should survive intact. When in doubt about product voice, re-read this slice.

## Slice 3 — Synthesis request

> "Wait no, try to puzzle the pieces together. We are trying to have an amazing agentic AI bundled into a practice management. I'm not really sure where to take the product direction but I am giving you little slices. But one thing is for sure, the preparer will have an AI that will be top of the line."

**The non-negotiable:** "the preparer will have an AI that is top of the line." That means tax depth equal to or exceeding what Accrual / Basis / Gelt / Deduction are building, even if Antonio never invokes it directly. The same engine has to power a 30-second message draft and a 90-minute return review with the same factual rigor.

## Slice 4 — Client portal added + competitive deep dive request

> "There will be a client portal web app that the taxpayers will use. The client can upload documents and chat with the tax preparer directly on there. That being said... hmm... in terms of the product direction that's only part of the picture that I see. We are trying to compete with TaxGPT and other AI native companies. TaxGPT recently came out with Agent Andrew and other very good features. Research the field of AI right now and where you see the most potential."

**What this added:** Two surfaces — Command Room (preparer) + Client Portal (taxpayer). The portal is **mediated by AI, gated by the preparer**. Taxpayer never interacts with autonomous AI. Every AI action is preparer-approved.

## Slice 5 — Predictive outcomes + Command Room + engineering hell

> "Blue J — Predictive outcome tool. Blue J is unique because it uses machine learning to predict the outcome of tax positions based on existing tax law and judicial precedent. ... I also want this in our product.
>
> What I'm kind of thinking is Docket is the command room for the entire tax workflow, if that's possible. You see where all the clients are at, what your practice needs, intelligent insights about the client and the practice and everything around it, but not only that, it can also do things via MCP servers. It is the command room. Sort of like OpenClaw where you operate a lot of different things from OpenClaw via integrations. What do you think? Is this engineering hell? If people develop MCP servers wouldn't it be better?
>
> Also we probably won't do WhatsApp."

**Locked here:**
- Outcome prediction service (Blue J partnership v1, native model on practice ledger by year 2)
- Command Room metaphor — single pane, AI commentary on every surface, command palette routes through MCP
- WhatsApp deferred (substrate is SMS + email + voice + portal chat)

## Slice 9 — Stripe Identity + tech-stack questions

> "What about using Claude Code CLI instead of direct API? So it's a subscription bill instead of a giant fat bill?"

> "What FluentOS does to make them SOC 2 certified (not actual SOC 2 but SOC 2 aligned) is they do AWS Bedrock for the AI so that the sensitive data never trains the model. Is this the move? Also my junior dev talked about using FastAPI and Python backend. Take as grain of salt but is this also a good idea? ... We are planning to add Stripe Identity KYC as it is needed for signing the 8879."

**Locked here:**
- Claude Code CLI as production inference REJECTED (against ToS, no SLA, can't multi-tenant)
- Bedrock deferred to per-tenant flag for compliance customers
- TypeScript end-to-end (Python rejected — junior dev's instinct comes from ML-tutorial bias)
- Stripe Identity for KYC before 8879 e-signing

## Slice 10 — Cost shock

> "Holy SHIT that is expensive. OK... I need this to be extremely cost effective. Max I can spend is like $100 or $200. Maybe we look for an alternative then. Maybe something open source. Maybe something like an enterprise version of OpenClaw somewhere out there... or a harness open source that can be customized. That is way too expensive. The best optimal path would be to develop it all in Claude Code. How do you think FluentOS did it?"

**Locked here:**
- Target: $50/mo Anthropic API spend (plus existing Claude Code Max $100/mo)
- Haiku-first testing, prompt caching, batch API, Computer Use deferred to week 7+
- Free-tier infrastructure until first paying customer
- Honest reframe: harness choice doesn't reduce inference cost; model + caching + mocking does
