[1/7]
Real Estate Professional Status under §469(c)(7) is the highest-leverage
escape from passive activity loss limitations for rental real estate
investors. It's also the most-audited.

Here is what actually works on substantiation.

[2/7]
The two tests:
1. > 50% of personal services in real property trades or businesses
2. > 750 hours during the tax year

Both. Not either. The IRS audit guide for Passive Activity Loss
explicitly tests for both prongs.

[3/7]
Contemporaneous time log is the load-bearing requirement per
§1.469-5T(f).

Acceptable:
- Calendar with property-specific entries
- Property management software logs (Buildium, AppFolio, Rentec Direct)
- Brokerage activity records
- Construction project schedules

[4/7]
Not acceptable:
- Hours reconstructed retroactively from "memory"
- Round-number hours (exactly 751)
- Hours without property-specific allocation
- Spreadsheet built after-the-fact with no underlying source records

[5/7]
The aggregation election under §1.469-9(g) is the second load-bearing
requirement.

Without it, material participation must be analyzed PER ACTIVITY
(each rental property is a separate activity by default).

With it, all rentals treated as a single activity for material
participation purposes.

[6/7]
The election is IRREVOCABLE absent FTB consent.

Once filed, it sticks for every subsequent year. Make sure the client
understands before filing.

Rev. Proc. 2010-13 governs the filing procedure.

- Hakkak (T.C. Memo 2020) — rejected; inadequate hours documentation
- Penley (T.C. Memo 2017) — rejected; W-2 full-time conflict

### Thread 2 — Self-rental rules (the trap that bites every S-Corp owner with rental property) (post Wed-Thu AM; 6 tweets)

```
[1/6]
Pattern I see weekly:

S-Corp owner also owns the building the S-Corp operates in. Rents
the building to the S-Corp. Treats rental income as passive →
tries to absorb other passive losses.

This is the §1.469-2(f)(6) trap.

[2/6]
§469(c)(2) makes rental real estate per-se passive. Default.

So an S-Corp owner with rental income looks like they have passive
income that can offset their passive losses (from, say, a passive
investment K-1 or a separate real estate investment).

The IRS closed this loophole.

[3/6]
Treas. Reg. §1.469-2(f)(6) recharacterizes self-rental NET INCOME
as non-passive when:

1. Taxpayer rents property to a trade or business
2. Taxpayer materially participates in that business

The income cannot be used to absorb unrelated passive losses.

[4/6]
But the asymmetry: self-rental LOSSES remain passive by default.

So a taxpayer who rents at below FMV (creating losses) is stuck —
losses limited under §469(i) phaseout.

The fix is the §1.469-4(d) grouping election.

[5/6]
Grouping makes the rental + operating business a single activity.

Material participation in the combined activity allows non-passive
treatment of BOTH income and loss.

The grouping must be "reasonable" per §1.469-4(c)(2). Operating
business operating IN the rental property is the classic reasonable
fact pattern.

[6/6]
Cases:
- Krukowski v. Comm'r (T.C. Memo 2000) — recharacterization upheld
- Carlos v. Comm'r (T.C. Memo 2010) — recharacterization upheld
- Senra v. Comm'r (T.C. Memo 2011) — grouping rejected as unreasonable

Get the grouping right at the start. Wrong election sticks for years.

#TaxTwitter
```
